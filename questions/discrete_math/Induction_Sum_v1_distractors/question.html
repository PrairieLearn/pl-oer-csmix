At $n=1$, $\sum_{j=0}^n \frac{1}{j(j+1)} = \frac{1}{1(1+1)} = \frac{1}{2}$. Also $\frac{n}{n+1} = \frac{1}{2}$. So the two sides of the equation are equal and $P(1)$ holds. By the inductive hypothesis $ \sum_{j=0}^k \frac{1}{j(j+1)} = \frac{k}{k+1}$, so removing the top term of our summation and then applying this we get $\sum_{j=0}^{k+1} \frac{1}{j(j+1)} = \frac{1}{(k+1)(k+2)} + \sum_{j=0}^{k} \frac{1}{j(j+1)} = \frac{1}{(k+1)(k+2)} + \frac{k}{k+1}$ Adding the two fractions together: $\frac{1}{(k+1)(k+2)} + \frac{k}{k+1} = \frac{1 + k(k+2)}{(k+1)(k+2)} = \frac{k^2 + 2k + 1}{(k+1)(k+2)} = \frac{(k+1)^2}{(k+1)(k+2)} = \frac{k+1}{k+2}$ So $\sum_{j=0}^{k+1} \frac{1}{j(j+1)} = \frac{k+1}{k+2}$, which is what we needed to show.
